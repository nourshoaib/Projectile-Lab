Introduction to Projectile Motion

Video credit: Khan Academy

What is Projectile Motion?

Projectile motion is the motion of an object thrown or projected into the air, with initial velocity, and subject only to its weight.

Key Characteristics:
  • Two-dimensional motion: Can be analyzed as two independent one-dimensional motions (horizontal and vertical)
  • Parabolic trajectory: The path is always a parabola (when air resistance is negligible)
  • Constant horizontal velocity: No acceleration in the horizontal direction (ax = 0)
  • Constant vertical acceleration: ay = -g [e.g. -9.8 m/s²] (downward)

Breaking Down the Motion

Horizontal Motion

The horizontal motion is uniform (constant velocity) because there are no horizontal forces acting (ignoring air resistance).

x(t) = v₀ₓ·t + x₀

Where:

  • x₀ = initial horizontal position
  • v₀ₓ = v₀·cosθ = initial horizontal velocity component
  • t = time

Vertical Motion

The vertical motion is uniformly accelerated motion due to gravity.

y(t) = - ½gt² + v₀ᵧ·t + y₀

Where:

  • y₀ = initial vertical position
  • v₀ᵧ = v₀·sinθ = initial vertical velocity component
  • g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s² downward)

Key Quantities and Examples

Time of Flight

The total time the projectile remains in the air.

T = [v₀·sinθ + √( (v₀·sinθ)² + 2g(y₀ - yground) )] / g

Special Case: Ground-to-Ground (y₀ = yground)

T = (2v₀·sinθ)/g

This simplifies when launched and landing at the same elevation.

Maximum Height

The highest vertical position reached by the projectile.

H = (v₀·sinθ)²/(2g) + y₀

Range

The horizontal distance traveled when the projectile returns to its initial vertical position.

R = (v₀²·sin(2θ))/g

Practical Example:

A ball is kicked with an initial velocity of 20 m/s at 30° above horizontal. Calculate:

  1. Time of flight
  2. Maximum height
  3. Range

Special Cases and Common Mistakes

Launch Angle Effects

  • 45° launch: Provides maximum range when launched from ground level
  • Complementary angles: 30° and 60° launches give the same range (when y₀ = yGround)
  • 90° launch: Pure vertical motion (range = 0)

Common Student Mistakes

Mixing up sin and cos: Remember v₀ₓ = v₀·cosθ (horizontal) and v₀ᵧ = v₀·sinθ (vertical)
Forgetting initial height: The equations change if the projectile doesn't start and end at the same vertical position
Ignoring vector nature: Velocity and acceleration are vectors - direction matters!

Advanced Considerations

While our calculator uses simplified physics (no air resistance), in reality:

  • Air resistance reduces range and maximum height
  • Spin affects trajectory (Magnus effect)
  • At very high speeds, curvature of Earth becomes significant

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does horizontal velocity remain constant?

In projectile motion (ignoring air resistance), there is no horizontal acceleration acting on the object. According to Newton's First Law, an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity unless acted upon by an external force.

What angle gives the maximum range?

When launched from ground level (y₀ = 0), the maximum range is achieved at a 45° launch angle. For other initial heights, the optimal angle depends on both the initial velocity and height.

How does air resistance affect projectile motion?

Air resistance reduces both horizontal and vertical components of velocity, resulting in:
  • Shorter range
  • Lower maximum height
  • Asymmetric trajectory (steeper descent)
Our calculator assumes no air resistance for simplicity.
